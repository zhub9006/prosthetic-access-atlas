# Combined Gap Analysis: Prosthetic & Orthotic Care in Underserved U.S. Regions

## Executive Summary

Analysis of three critically underserved regions reveals a devastating convergence: populations with the **highest amputation risk** have the **lowest access to prosthetic and orthotic care**. No clinical trials are registering from these regions, meaning the evidence base for treating them is practically nonexistent.

---

## Region 1: Pocahontas County, West Virginia

- **Overall Livability Score**: 3.6/10
- **Healthcare Score**: **0/10** CRITICAL
- **Healthcare Facilities Within 30km**: **ZERO**
- **Walkability**: 0

### What This Means:
No hospital, clinic, pharmacy, or medical provider of any kind exists within 30km. Residents must travel at least 30+ km for basic healthcare. Prosthetic and orthotic services - already rare even in urban areas - are completely absent. WV has some of the nation's highest rates of diabetes and peripheral vascular disease, making amputation a common outcome for residents who can't access preventive care.

### Nearest Cities:
- Marlinton, WV (~10 km) - tiny town, no specialists
- Lewisburg, WV (~35 km NE) - nearest credible medical hub

### Research Gap:
Zero clinical trials tie this area. No one is studying how to deliver prosthetic care to populations like this.

## Region 2: Breathitt County, Kentucky

- **Overall Livability Score**: 4.8/10
- **Healthcare Score**: 5.5/10 (limited)
- **Healthcare Facilities Within 30km**: Limited (small regional hospitals)
- **Walkability**: 0

### What This Means:
While some basic healthcare exists (Jackson, KY ~8km), there are no dedicated prosthetic/orthotic clinics. Patients need specialized fitting, adjustments, and rehabilitation - none of which are available locally. Appalachian Kentucky has among the highest rates of diabetes, obesity, and smoking-related disease in the nation. Car-dependency in a region with high poverty rates = impossible access.

### Nearest Cities:
- Jackson, KY (~8 km) - county seat, general hospital
- Hazard, KY (~27 km) - larger regional center

### Research Gap:
Zero trials registered from Appalachian KY. Research focus is on urban academic centers.

## Region 3: Mississippi Delta

- **Overall Livability Score**: Estimated ~3.0/10
- **Healthcare Score**: Estimated ~2/10
- **Healthcare Facilities Within 50km**: Virtually none for prosthetic/orthotic needs
- **Walking/Mobility**: Low

### What This Means:
The Delta is one of the most economically distressed regions in the entire United States. Diabetes and obesity rates are astronomical. Amputation rates are among the highest in the nation. Yet specialized prosthetic and orthotic care is essentially absent - the nearest providers are in Jackson, MS (~50 km) or Memphis, TN (~120 km). For many residents without reliable transportation, this is effectively unreachable.

### Nearest Cities:
- Greenville, MS (~0 km) - county seat, general hospital (no prosthetics)
- Cleveland, MS (~35 km)
- Jackson, MS (~50 km)
- Memphis, TN (~120 km)

### Research Gap:
Zero trials from the Mississippi Delta. A region with the highest amputation rates in the US has virtually no research presence.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. The Access-Research Paradox
The regions with the highest need for prosthetic care have the least research addressing their reality. Clinical trials concentrate in urban academic centers (US: 336 trials, but in cities). Rural Appalachia and the Delta are invisible in the prosthetic research literature.

### 2. The Transportation Barrier
All three regions score 0 on walkability. Prosthetic care requires multiple in-person visits for fitting, alignment, gait training, and adjustments. Without reliable transportation or public transit, these visits become insurmountable.

### 3. The Diabetes-Amputation Pipeline
West Virginia, Appalachian Kentucky, and the Mississippi Delta all rank among the top US regions for diabetes prevalence. Diabetes → peripheral neuropathy → foot ulcers → infection → amputation. This pipeline is well-documented but poorly addressed by research or care infrastructure.

### 4. What Would Intervention Look Like?

1. **Mobile Prosthetic Clinics**: Van-based services that rotate through underserved counties
2. **Telehealth Prosthetic fittings**: Using 3D scanning and video consultation
3. **Community Health Worker Programs**: Training locals to provide basic prosthetic maintenance
4. **Research Trials in Rural Settings**: The PROINGA Gaza study shows how urgent contexts can drive innovation - rural America is an equally urgent context
5. **Insurance Advocacy**: AMTRAC and prosthetic coverage varies wildly by state; these regions need policy attention
